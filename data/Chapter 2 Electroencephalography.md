# Chapter 2: Electroencephalography (EEG)

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive technique used to measure the electrical activity of the brain. The human brain contains billions of neurons that communicate through electrical impulses. When large groups of neurons become active simultaneously, they generate electrical signals that can be detected on the scalp using specialized sensors called electrodes. EEG records these signals over time, providing valuable information about brain function, cognitive processes, and neurological conditions.

The history of EEG dates back to the early twentieth century when scientists began exploring the electrical properties of the brain. The first successful recording of human brain activity was achieved by the German psychiatrist Hans Berger in 1924. Berger demonstrated that electrical signals generated by the brain could be measured from the scalp and recorded as waveforms. His work laid the foundation for modern neurophysiology and brain-computer interface research.

An EEG system consists of several key components. The most visible components are electrodes placed on the scalp. These electrodes detect tiny voltage fluctuations produced by neuronal activity. The detected signals are amplified because brain signals are extremely weak, typically ranging from a few microvolts to hundreds of microvolts. After amplification, the signals are digitized and processed by computers for visualization and analysis. Modern EEG systems may contain anywhere from a few electrodes to more than 256 channels, depending on the application's requirements.

Electrode placement follows standardized systems to ensure consistency across studies and clinical examinations. The most widely used standard is the International 10–20 System. In this system, electrodes are positioned according to percentages of skull dimensions. Labels such as F, T, C, P, and O represent different brain regions, including frontal, temporal, central, parietal, and occipital areas. This standardized arrangement enables researchers and clinicians to compare recordings obtained from different individuals and institutions.

EEG signals are typically analyzed in terms of frequency bands. Different frequency ranges are associated with different brain states and cognitive activities. Delta waves, ranging from approximately 0.5 to 4 Hz, are commonly observed during deep sleep. Theta waves, ranging from 4 to 8 Hz, are associated with drowsiness, meditation, and certain memory processes. Alpha waves, ranging from 8 to 13 Hz, often appear when a person is awake but relaxed with closed eyes. Beta waves, ranging from 13 to 30 Hz, are associated with active thinking, concentration, and problem-solving. Gamma waves, typically above 30 Hz, are linked to higher cognitive functions such as attention, perception, and information integration.

One of the major advantages of EEG is its excellent temporal resolution. Brain activity can be recorded on a millisecond timescale, allowing researchers to observe rapid neural processes as they occur. This makes EEG particularly useful for studying perception, attention, language processing, decision-making, and motor control. Compared with imaging techniques such as MRI, EEG provides a much more detailed view of how brain activity changes over time.

Despite its strengths, EEG has certain limitations. The spatial resolution of EEG is relatively low because electrical signals must pass through brain tissue, cerebrospinal fluid, the skull, and the scalp before reaching the electrodes. These structures distort and spread the signals, making it difficult to precisely identify the source of neural activity. Additionally, EEG recordings are susceptible to noise from eye movements, muscle activity, electrical equipment, and environmental interference.

Signal preprocessing is therefore an essential step in EEG analysis. Raw EEG data often contains artifacts that can obscure meaningful brain signals. Common preprocessing techniques include filtering, baseline correction, artifact removal, and signal normalization. Researchers may use methods such as Independent Component Analysis (ICA) to separate brain activity from unwanted noise sources. Effective preprocessing improves data quality and enhances the reliability of subsequent analyses.

Machine Learning has become increasingly important in EEG research. EEG data is high-dimensional, noisy, and complex, making it a suitable candidate for advanced computational methods. Machine learning algorithms can identify patterns in EEG signals that may not be apparent through manual inspection. Classification algorithms are frequently used to distinguish between different mental states, detect neurological disorders, or recognize specific cognitive tasks.

Deep Learning has further expanded the possibilities of EEG analysis. Neural network architectures such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and Transformer-based models can automatically learn features from raw EEG signals. These models have achieved promising results in applications such as emotion recognition, seizure detection, sleep stage classification, cognitive workload assessment, and brain-computer interfaces.

Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) represent one of the most exciting applications of EEG technology. A BCI enables direct communication between the brain and external devices. EEG signals can be analyzed in real time to control computers, robotic arms, wheelchairs, and other assistive technologies. Such systems are particularly valuable for individuals with severe motor impairments. By interpreting neural activity, BCIs can provide alternative communication and control mechanisms without requiring physical movement.

EEG also plays a crucial role in clinical medicine. Neurologists use EEG to diagnose and monitor conditions such as epilepsy, sleep disorders, encephalopathy, and certain brain injuries. During epilepsy diagnosis, EEG recordings can reveal abnormal electrical discharges associated with seizures. Sleep laboratories use EEG to identify different stages of sleep and investigate disorders such as insomnia, narcolepsy, and sleep apnea. Continuous EEG monitoring is also employed in intensive care units to assess brain function in critically ill patients.

In cognitive neuroscience, EEG is widely used to study how the brain processes information. Researchers examine event-related potentials (ERPs), which are specific EEG responses triggered by sensory, cognitive, or motor events. ERPs provide insights into attention, memory, language comprehension, decision-making, and emotional processing. Because ERPs can be measured with high temporal precision, they offer a powerful tool for understanding the timing of neural processes.

The future of EEG research is closely linked to advances in machine learning, wearable technology, and neuroengineering. Modern wearable EEG devices are becoming smaller, more comfortable, and more affordable. These developments are expanding the use of EEG beyond laboratories and hospitals into everyday environments. Combined with artificial intelligence, EEG has the potential to transform healthcare, human-computer interaction, mental health monitoring, and personalized neurotechnology.

Electroencephalography remains one of the most important tools for studying brain activity. Its ability to capture neural dynamics in real time, combined with modern computational techniques, makes it a cornerstone of neuroscience, clinical diagnosis, and brain-computer interface development. As technology continues to evolve, EEG is expected to play an increasingly significant role in understanding the human brain and creating innovative applications that bridge the gap between neural activity and intelligent systems.
